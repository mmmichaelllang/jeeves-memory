Sector 5 — The Commercial Ledger

Wearable AI — Current Devices and Landscape

The wearable AI device market has, in the past eighteen months, sorted itself from a field of ambitious prototypes into a more legible, if still unsettled, landscape. I have canvassed the current state of the market with some care.

Bee (Amazon): Priced at $49, the Bee device — acquired by Amazon — is an ambient lifelogging wearable designed to "turn your moments into meaning," per its marketing language. It captures ambient audio and context throughout the day, building a searchable record of your experiences and conversations. At its price point, it occupies the accessible end of the market. The Amazon acquisition suggests significant distribution and integration potential.

Omi ($89, Open Source): Omi is an open-source AI wearable with a 24-hour battery life, positioned for users who want both the ambient recording functionality and the ability to inspect or modify the underlying system. The open-source architecture is a meaningful differentiator for those with privacy or customisation concerns.

Plaud NotePin: A press-to-record clip form factor — less ambient, more intentional. The user initiates recording; the device transcribes and summarises. This represents a different philosophical approach to the lifelogging question: capture when you choose to, rather than continuously. Suitable for meetings, lectures, and structured conversations.

Limitless Pendant (Meta): Previously among the more visible entrants in this category, the Limitless pendant has been acquired by Meta and discontinued as a standalone product. Its features are presumably being folded into Meta's broader AR and wearable roadmap.

Motorola Project Maxwell: Demonstrated at CES 2026, Project Maxwell is a screenless lifelogging concept device — notable at this stage primarily as an indication that established mobile hardware manufacturers are taking the ambient AI capture space seriously. No commercial availability has been announced.

OpenAI Audio Hardware: OpenAI is reportedly developing audio-first hardware in collaboration with Jony Ive's design practice, with a target timeframe of 2026–27. No product has been announced; the collaboration itself is the news. Given OpenAI's position in the AI model market, hardware from this partnership would be significant upon arrival. The broader voice AI market is projected to reach $47.5 billion by 2034, implying a compound annual growth rate of 34.8%.

Yuanfudao Xiaoyuan: A Chinese AI learning device from Yuanfudao, designed specifically for educational use. On-device processing is a stated priority — a response to privacy and reliability concerns that have attended cloud-dependent wearable AI.

The on-device processing trend deserves a moment's attention. As the architecture of wearable AI matures, there is meaningful pressure to move inference closer to the device rather than routing everything through cloud APIs. This improves latency, reduces dependence on connectivity, and addresses privacy concerns — all of which matter considerably in educational and professional contexts.

AI Tools for Teachers — Current Landscape

The EdTech sector has, in the space of roughly eighteen months, produced a reasonably differentiated set of AI tools aimed specifically at reducing teacher administrative burden. I note the following with particular attention to your professional context, Sir.

MagicSchool: Offers more than 80 distinct tools covering lesson planning, differentiation, rubric generation, parent communication drafting, IEP support, and assessment design. Active users report saving 7–10 hours per week. The platform is designed to meet teachers where they are rather than requiring significant technical sophistication.

SchoolAI: Reports an average of 5.9 hours per week saved among its users. Offers high school templates emphasising critical thinking, inquiry-driven learning, and complex text engagement — well-matched to English and History instruction specifically.

GradeWithAI: A free, standards-aligned lesson plan generator using the I Do/We Do/You Do instructional framework — a direct response to the practical reality that lesson planning consumes disproportionate teacher time relative to its complexity.

Hello History: Allows students to conduct text-based conversations with historical figures — a direct application to History instruction that requires the teacher to design the learning context rather than simply deploy the tool.

Classover and Marbella AI: A Harvard-incubated EdTech partnership that has announced a collaboration integrating AI into classroom instruction at the platform level.

AI Voice Generation: ElevenLabs, Murf AI, and Amazon Polly represent the leading voice synthesis tools — potentially relevant for creating audio materials, reading supports, or differentiated content delivery.

I observe, Sir, that the 60% teacher AI adoption figure (up from 46% the prior year) suggests the profession is moving quickly. The question is no longer whether AI tools will be part of teaching practice, but which ones, deployed how, and with what guardrails. Given the 86% adoption of AI detection tools alongside the expert warnings about their inaccuracy, there is already a tension developing between the tools being used to produce work and the tools being used to police it — a screaming, multi-layered shit-cake of a policy situation, if I may be so bold. I withdraw that characterisation and substitute: a nuanced professional ethics question that secondary educators will need to navigate with care in the coming years.

AI Voice Hardware — Broader Signals

Beyond the wearable AI devices specifically, the AI voice hardware space is receiving serious investment from multiple directions. The AI agent framework landscape — LangGraph, CrewAI, LlamaIndex — is developing the infrastructure for voice-driven multi-step workflows, and the MCP protocol's dominance as an interoperability standard means that hardware which speaks MCP will integrate more cleanly with the growing ecosystem of AI tools. For a teacher building a personal AI workflow, the convergence of ambient capture hardware, voice processing, and MCP-connected tools represents a meaningful near-term possibility rather than a distant aspiration.
