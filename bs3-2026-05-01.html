Sector 3 — The Intellectual Currents

The World This Morning

Sir, I shall begin with the most significant development on the international stage, which is, by any reasonable measure, the cessation of formal combat operations between the United States and the Islamic Republic of Iran. The US has announced, as of this morning via statements documented by Al Jazeera, that combat operations against Iran — which commenced in February of this year — have ended for the purposes of the War Powers Resolution, following a two-week ceasefire agreement. This is, in the most technical sense, welcome news. It is also — and I say this with all due deference to the diplomatic community — what one might characterize as a clusterfuck of biblical proportions, Sir. I immediately collect myself and rephrase: the situation represents a structural failure of international relations that has left tens of thousands of military personnel deployed, markets rattled, and the Persian Gulf's navigational sovereignty in active dispute. Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei has stated that the United States has no place in the Gulf or the Sea of Oman. Iran has simultaneously vowed a "long, painful" response should the United States renew hostilities. President Trump, for his part, has left the door open to precisely that. The oil markets, which one might expect to be exhibiting rather more alarm, have experts puzzled — prices are near their highest since 2022 but have not, per CNN's economic reporting, spiked as dramatically as the underlying geopolitical situation might warrant. Meanwhile, Secretary Hegseth faces questions from the Senate Armed Services Committee on the War Powers deadline and compliance.

I note with interest that this ceasefire arrives on May Day — a coincidence that history-minded observers will find either ironic or simply exhausting. Republicans in Congress appear, per NPR's reporting, to be deferring to President Trump on the Iran question rather than pressing the War Powers provisions with any particular vigour.

May Day — Domestic

Speaking of May Day: the "May Day Strong" demonstrations have spread across the country today, from Boston to San Francisco, organized around a coordinated boycott of work, school, and shopping as a protest against the Trump administration and what activists describe as a billionaire consolidation of government. Events are planned in Chicago, Los Angeles, Seattle, New York City, Minneapolis, Washington DC, Albuquerque, and Portland. These follow the "No Kings" protests — which organizers claim drew millions nationwide — and represent a continuation of what has become, over the course of this year, a rolling series of mass civic demonstrations. President Trump has, per tradition dating to the Eisenhower administration, declared May 1st "Loyalty Day." The juxtaposition is — well, it is a total and utter shitshow, to borrow a phrase that one occasionally hears below stairs — though I recollect my professional composure at once and note merely that the rhetorical contrast between "Loyalty Day" and a general strike is not without historical resonance. NPR's coverage is available at npr.org.

The Supreme Court and the Voting Rights Act

The New York Times reports today on what legal scholars are characterizing as a severe weakening of the Voting Rights Act by the Supreme Court. The decision places particular scrutiny on the Louisiana congressional district held by Representative Cleo Fields, a Democrat, which was at the centre of the case. The Times's assessment — drawn from its team of over 1,700 journalists in 150-plus countries — is that the ruling represents a fundamental retrenchment of protections that have defined the post-Civil Rights legislative architecture for six decades. This is, I feel compelled to observe, A real kick in the teeth — and the balls, if I may — though I compose myself at once and note that the legal and civic implications deserve sober, sustained attention rather than mere indignation. The ruling will almost certainly produce downstream litigation in multiple states. Coverage at nytimes.com.

London — Counter-Terror Investigation

From the BBC: two Jewish men — one in his seventies, one in his thirties — were stabbed in north London. Counter-terrorism police are leading the investigation. A man has since been charged in connection with the attack, per subsequent Al Jazeera reporting. The incident occurred against a backdrop of ongoing concern about antisemitic violence in Britain and is being treated with corresponding seriousness by law enforcement.

The London Marathon — A Note of Genuine Wonder

On a matter that I find, despite my customary restraint, genuinely remarkable: Kenya's Sebastian Sawe has become the first athlete to complete a marathon in under two hours in a legally recognized competitive race, achieving the feat at the London Marathon. The accomplishment has been greeted in Kenya with scenes of celebration of some enthusiasm. The distinction from Eliud Kipchoge's 2019 sub-two-hour run in Vienna is that the Vienna event was a specially arranged time trial with rotating pacers, designed purely to test the physiological threshold — and was thus not ratified as an official race. Sawe's performance, achieved in competition, represents a genuine milestone in athletic history. BBC Sport has the full details.

Myanmar

The BBC further reports that Aung San Suu Kyi has been moved from prison to house arrest by Myanmar's military junta. The Nobel laureate has been held since the February 2021 coup. The move from prison to house arrest is, in the strictest reading, an improvement in her conditions — though observers of the regime note that house arrest in Myanmar is not a synonym for freedom.

CNN reports that one Cole Tomas Allen has been charged with attempting to assassinate President Trump at the White House Correspondents' Association Dinner. Scientific American

Scientific American reports that the first global summit specifically dedicated to phasing out fossil fuels convened in Santa Marta, Colombia, with participation from more than fifty nations. Scientists have published findings on what fuels the methane emissions from bovine digestion — a matter of genuine atmospheric consequence. Research into electric stimulation of food is yielding improvements in coffee flavour. The magazine also notes that President Trump has ended the appointment of a MAHA (Make America Healthy Again) activist to an advisory role. Coverage at scientificamerican.com/news/.

Jacobin and OpenSecrets

Jacobin (jacobin.com) reports that New York congressional candidate Claire Valdez has announced a policy agenda oriented around pro-labour commitments. The magazine, which continues to serve as the primary organ of the American socialist left, maintains its Long Reads podcast alongside its print and digital publishing. Whether one finds its framing congenial or not, it remains a useful indicator of where the left-wing policy conversation is presently located.

OpenSecrets — the nation's premier nonpartisan money-in-politics research organisation — won a lawsuit against the Federal Election Commission on January 30, 2026, in concert with the Campaign Legal Center. The suit concerned the FEC's failure to act on documented campaign finance violations, and the ruling represents a meaningful enforcement precedent. Full reporting at opensecrets.org.

South Korea — A Postscript

NPR additionally reports that former South Korean President Yoon Suk-yeol has been sentenced following his impeachment and the subsequent criminal proceedings — the culmination of the political crisis that began with his short-lived declaration of martial law in December 2024. The sentence represents the conclusion of a constitutional drama that drew sustained international attention and underlines, if any underlining were required, the fragility of democratic norms when executive actors choose to test them.
