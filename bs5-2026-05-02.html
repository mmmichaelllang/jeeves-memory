Sector 5 — The Commercial Ledger

Wearable AI Devices — Current Market

The wearable and ambient AI hardware sector has, in the past six months, clarified into a set of distinct product categories, each with a different primary use case. I present the current landscape, Sir, as it bears on your stated interests in both personal productivity and classroom application.

Bee (acquired by Amazon; $49) operates as an ambient lifelogging device — passively recording all-day audio and converting it into summaries and reminders. It has been called the top pick for affordability in its category, and Amazon's acquisition suggests the model will either scale with broader ecosystem integration or be quietly retired into Alexa's infrastructure. At $49, the entry cost for evaluation is modest.

Limitless (acquired by Meta; $99, currently discontinued) was a professional meeting assistant with speaker identification, a 100-hour battery, and synchronisation with Zoom and Microsoft Teams. Meta's acquisition has produced a discontinuation notice, but the company is expected to reintroduce a version of the product integrated with its own AI infrastructure. This is a device to monitor rather than purchase in its present form.

Omi ($89; open-source; available in necklace and headband form factors; 24-hour battery; local and cloud processing options) is the most technically interesting of the current offerings, in my assessment. The open-source architecture means the processing pipeline can be inspected and modified — a non-trivial advantage for anyone with privacy concerns about all-day ambient recording, or with specific integration needs. Local processing eliminates the cloud-dependency risk.

The Plaud NotePin clips to clothing and focuses on structured note-taking rather than ambient lifelogging — a narrower use case, but one that may be appropriate for meeting-intensive contexts. At CES 2026, Lenovo and Motorola showed the Project Maxwell concept — a screenless lifelogging device that attracted considerable attention as a demonstration of where the category is heading, though it has not yet reached consumer release.

The voice AI hardware market is projected at $47.5 billion by 2034, with a compound annual growth rate of 34.8 percent. The industry-wide directional trend is from cloud processing to on-device processing, with microphone arrays of three to eight microphones doing beamforming — directional audio isolation — as a hardware-level feature. This reduces cloud dependency, latency, and data exposure simultaneously.

OpenAI Audio-First Hardware

OpenAI is, according to current reporting, developing an audio-first hardware device designed by Jony Ive — the industrial designer responsible for the canonical Apple hardware aesthetic of the past three decades. The device is expected to arrive in 2026, with form factors under exploration ranging from smart speaker to eyewear, all unified by a voice-first, screen-absent design philosophy. This is paired with what OpenAI describes as new audio-based ChatGPT models optimised for the interface. The device's significance, if it reaches market in anything like the form described, is that it would represent the first genuinely new computing interface paradigm since the smartphone — not a refinement of screen-and-keyboard, but a replacement of screen entirely. Whether OpenAI can execute on this is a separate question from whether the concept is directionally correct, and I would submit that the concept almost certainly is.

AI Tools for Educators — Teacher Productivity

I have consolidated the relevant findings from the EdTech research for practical reference, Sir, given the proximity of your career transition.

MagicSchool offers over eighty tools specific to educator workflows — lesson plans, IEP drafting, rubric generation, parent email composition, differentiated materials, and Socratic seminar structures for secondary grades. The time savings data (seven to ten hours per week) comes from educator self-reporting, which one treats with the usual statistical caution, but the order of magnitude is consistent across multiple reporting sources.

SchoolAI provides secondary-specific templates (grades 9–12) focused on critical thinking, inquiry-driven discussion, complex text analysis, and college-readiness scaffolding. The platform's reported average of 5.9 hours saved per week translates to approximately six weeks per school year — time that, in a high school English context, is most usefully redirected toward actual feedback on student writing rather than administrative overhead.

GradeWithAI's History Lesson Plan Generator is free, produces standards-aligned plans using the Gradual Release of Responsibility framework, and generates objectives, activities, differentiation strategies, and assessment criteria simultaneously. For a teacher entering a new district with unfamiliar pacing guides, this represents a substantial reduction in initial planning burden.

Brisk Teaching is ranked among the top EdTech learning resources for 2026. Edmentum offers digital curriculum specifically designed for differentiation. Figjam has replaced Google Jamboard as the standard AI-assisted digital whiteboard. Genially produces interactive lessons with embedded audio and clickable content at no cost to educators.

JetLearn's 2026 ranking of forty EdTech tools covers AI platforms, VR and AR, assessment, content creation, collaborative tools, classroom management, and accessibility — a useful taxonomy for systematic evaluation. The 74 percent of teachers who report using AI for differentiation, and the 86 percent using AI detection tools (which experts flag as unreliable, particularly for neurodivergent writers and non-native English speakers), suggest that the professional community is adopting these tools faster than the research on their efficacy — or their failure modes — can keep pace.

I would note, Sir, that a secondary English teacher who enters a district with fluency in both the pedagogical uses and the documented limitations of these tools is positioned rather differently than one who arrives either ignorant of them or uncritically enthusiastic. The market, in my experience, consistently rewards that particular species of informed scepticism.
