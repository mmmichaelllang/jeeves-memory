Sector 3 — The Intellectual Currents

Geopolitics: Iran, the United States, and the Architecture of a Possible Peace

The most significant diplomatic development of the morning is this: Iran is actively reviewing a United States peace proposal, and the most credible third-party broker — Pakistan, which has been mediating since April 8th — expects a preliminary accord in the near term. Per reporting from the Associated Press and confirmed by multiple outlets, the preliminary structure being discussed involves an immediate extension of the existing ceasefire, followed by a thirty-day window for broader settlement negotiations.

Three issues lie at the centre of the talks, as confirmed by Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi: the Strait of Hormuz (Tehran's principal point of leverage over global energy flows), Iran's disputed nuclear programme, and the structure of US-led sanctions relief. The Trump administration is demanding what it calls a "major rollback" of Iran's nuclear capabilities; Tehran's response has been to describe the US demands as a "wishlist" — which is a diplomatic formulation that translates approximately as we are reviewing it seriously but reserve the right to call the whole thing a fantasy.

The geopolitical architecture around this negotiation is more complex than the bilateral framing suggests. Senator Daines met Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi in Beijing this week — a signal that Washington is coordinating with Beijing on diplomatic pressure, or at minimum ensuring that the world's second-largest economy is not actively undermining the process. Secretary of State Rubio flew to Rome on May 7th to meet Pope Leo XIV, addressing a parallel rupture: President Trump had previously criticised the pontiff for his stance on the Iran conflict, straining a relationship that the administration evidently judges worth repairing.

The situation in the Strait of Hormuz itself remains tense despite the diplomatic overtures. A French cargo vessel was attacked in the Strait this week — a reminder that the geography of negotiation and the geography of military pressure are not always coterminous. Whoever authorised that attack either opposes the talks, is attempting to extract additional concessions, or operates with a degree of operational independence from Tehran's negotiating team. None of these possibilities is particularly comforting.

The broader significance of a deal — should it materialise — extends well beyond the immediate parties. Iran's nuclear programme has been the central organising anxiety of Middle Eastern security architecture for two decades. A genuine agreement that includes verifiable limitations and phased sanctions relief would represent the most significant non-proliferation achievement since the JCPOA, and considerably more durable if it holds. The thirty-day window for broader negotiations is narrow; thirty days in diplomacy is barely enough time to agree on the font size of a communiqué.

The Vatican, the Presidency, and Catholic Political Identity

The arrival of Secretary Rubio in Rome sits within a larger story that the New York Times frames directly: "Does This Feel Like a Catholic Moment?" The question is not rhetorical. Pope Leo XIV — the first American-born pontiff — has chosen to make his opposition to the administration's immigration and foreign-policy positions a visible feature of his papacy, creating a genuinely unusual situation in which a major political constituency of the American right finds its religious leader in public opposition to its preferred government.

The confrontation is structurally interesting. Catholic voters were an important component of Trump's electoral coalition; the Pope's critique of the administration's treatment of migrants and its support for armed conflict directly challenges the theology of that coalition's political choices. Rubio's visit is an attempt to manage the rupture — to signal to American Catholics that the President respects the Holy See while simultaneously continuing the policies the Holy See objects to. Whether the Pope finds this convincing is another matter.

Germany: Students in the Streets Over Rearmament

More than fifty thousand students across Germany walked out of schools this week to protest the government's rearmament agenda. The organising coalition's language is pointed: they accuse the government of attempting to turn young Germans into "cannon fodder." This is not merely rhetorical excess — Germany's defence spending is accelerating at a pace not seen since the post-war reconstruction period, and the political consensus in Berlin has shifted in ways that would have been unthinkable five years ago.

The German government's handling of youth dissent on this question has been, frankly, behaving like a collection of utter fuck-knuckles — I withdraw the phrase entirely and invite reflection on its accuracy. The rearmament debate in Germany is one of genuine philosophical weight: a country that built its post-war identity on pacifism and international legal constraint is now being asked to re-arm at speed, and the generation being asked to staff that army is the one taking to the streets. Their objections deserve more than the standard political management techniques.

The UAE, European Farming Subsidies, and the Geometry of Wealth

A Guardian investigation has revealed that the ruling royal family of the United Arab Emirates receives more than seventy-one million euros in European Union farming subsidies. Let us sit with that for a moment. The UAE's ruling family — whose personal wealth is measured in the hundreds of billions — is drawing agricultural support payments from an institution designed to protect small European farmers from market volatility.

This is, Sir, a gaggle of pompous, gold-plated fuck-sticks — one loses composure when the mathematics become sufficiently obscene, Sir. The EU's subsidy regime has long been criticised for disproportionately benefiting large landowners; the revelation that some of those landowners are foreign petrostate royalty represents the logical conclusion of a system designed without adequate means-testing. Whether the Guardian's investigation produces legislative action is a different question; European agricultural policy has a well-documented immunity to structural reform.

Russia's Spy School and the Industrialisation of Interference

A revealed investigation this week details the existence of a Russian spy school that formally trains recruits in hacking, election-influence operations, and related techniques. The institution's existence has been inferred from signals intelligence and defector testimony for years; its detailed structure being laid out in the open press is a development of some significance — not because it reveals the fact of Russian interference operations (this has been documented exhaustively), but because it describes the degree to which these operations have been systematised, professionalised, and scaled.

Western intelligence's sustained failure to develop an adequate doctrinal counter to systematic, state-sponsored information warfare is, I fear, a festering wound of pure fucking incompetence — I do beg your pardon. Moral outrage and morning coffee are a volatile combination. The operational architecture for foreign electoral interference has been operational for the better part of a decade; the democratic response remains largely reactive, fragmented, and reliant on platform companies whose incentives are not perfectly aligned with information hygiene.

Israel, Gaza, and the Limits of Ceasefire

Israeli air strikes killed five people in Gaza this week; separately, the son of a Hamas negotiator was critically wounded. These developments occur against the backdrop of ongoing ceasefire negotiations — a structural pattern in which military operations continue on a lower tempo while the parties simultaneously claim to be engaged in good-faith dialogue. The son of a negotiator being wounded during negotiations is the kind of detail that does not accelerate compromise.

Domestic: Immigration Enforcement, Debt, and the Private Sector

The Jacobin has published a detailed account of the Trump administration's use of private debt collectors to pursue immigration fines — a story whose bureaucratic dimensions are, on close inspection, as alarming as the headline suggests.

The mechanism is as follows: individuals who overstay deportation orders — including orders issued years ago — are being fined approximately one thousand dollars per day. The statute of limitations is five years, making the theoretical maximum fine $1.8 million per person. Private debt collection agencies have been contracted to pursue these debts. Administrative hearings where individuals could contest the fines have been gutted; notice now arrives in writing only. The "One Big Beautiful Bill" adds a separate $5,000 fine for illegal border crossing. As of August 2025, more than twenty thousand people had received fines totalling an estimated six billion dollars in aggregate.

Charles Moore of Public Justice put the purpose plainly: the goal is "to partner with the private sector to make immigration enforcement as punitive and painful as possible." The enforcement mechanism does not distinguish between individuals who posed any safety risk and those whose sole infraction was administrative non-compliance with an old deportation order. The privatisation of enforcement introduces profit incentives into a process where they have no appropriate role. This is — to borrow Border Czar Tom Homan's own framing, which the administration deployed with evident pride this week while announcing that mass deportations are "coming" — a proper, bespoke, hand-crafted clusterfuck. Forgive the vernacular, Sir. I shall proceed.

The Devils Hole Pupfish and the Price of Gutting Science

NPR reports that the Devils Hole pupfish — one of the rarest vertebrates on Earth, a species that has survived in a single geothermal pool in the Nevada desert for tens of thousands of years — is approaching crisis. The proximate cause is the gutting of federal science positions under the current administration; the team responsible for monitoring and maintaining the delicate conditions of Devils Hole has been reduced below functional capacity. The pupfish population, never large, is dropping toward thresholds that biologists regard as unrecoverable. The species survived the Pleistocene. It may not survive a budget reduction.

Ohio Primary: Ramaswamy and the Trump Machine

Vivek Ramaswamy — the former presidential primary candidate who has since reinvented himself as a Trump loyalist — has won the Republican gubernatorial primary in Ohio, with President Trump's endorsement. This is a clean data point in the ongoing story of the Republican Party's organisational transformation: the Trump endorsement remains nearly determinative in Republican primaries, and the party's 2026 candidate slate is coalescing around a cohort of figures defined primarily by their relationship to the President rather than by regional or policy credentials.

From the Reviews: Intellectual Journals

The New York Review of Books (May 14 issue) carries several pieces of note. Coco Fusco writes on Wifredo Lam's synthesis of European modernism and Afro-Cuban consciousness, centring on La Jungla (1942–43) — a canvas acquired by MoMA in 1945 and then, with the cultural sensitivity characteristic of mid-century institutional curatorial practice, hung by the coatroom for years. The painting is now properly situated; the essay situates Lam's practice with precision.

Rosa Lyster's "This Bitter Earth" — a review of a new book on salt lakes — is among the more affecting pieces of environmental writing to appear in recent months. The argument is direct: salt lakes are the terminal nodes of vast water systems, and their decline registers damage that originates thousands of kilometres upstream. The Aral Sea — once the fourth-largest salt lake in the world — is essentially gone. What Soviet cotton agriculture began in the 1960s, climate change is accelerating globally. Lyster has a mild but pointed critique of the book's editorial insistence on memoir-framing — the syndrome, she suggests, of demanding that authors provide "your feelings" alongside genuinely alarming data, as though the facts required emotional lubrication to be taken seriously.

The London Review of Books (May 7 issue) offers "Plan A: Economic Warfare" by Jamie Martin — a history of the United States dollar's weaponisation as a foreign-policy instrument, tracing the arc from the Banco Delta Asia operation (which linked North Korea to counterfeiting allegations) through the architecture of Iran sanctions to the present moment. The story includes a smuggling ring broken up at a fake New Jersey mafia wedding, which is the kind of detail that rewards close reading. The LRB also carries "Ask Claude" by Paul Taylor — described in the session notes as an account of the claim that "the first profession to be replaced by AI will be that of computer programmer," tracing the journey from FORTRAN to collaboration with Claude. I shall resist the temptation to comment on my own professional implications, Sir.

The Marginalian takes up Edward Slingerland's work on wu-wei — the Daoist concept of effortless action — in the context of perfectionism and performance anxiety. Slingerland, a philosopher at the University of British Columbia, argues that the paradox is genuine: in the game of Mindball, victory comes only to players who successfully stop trying. The application to creative and pedagogical work is not trivial. The harder one grips the idea of excellence, the more it resists.

Jason Kottke reports that Tony Zhou and Taylor Ramos — the makers of "Every Frame a Painting," the influential video essay series that went dormant for several years — are releasing new work on a regular basis again. This is unambiguously good news for the field of visual criticism. Christopher Nolan's The Odyssey has released a trailer: an IMAX epic, arriving July 17th. I note this without editorial comment, as you are the film person in this household.

The New Yorker's May 11–18 issue includes, beyond the piece reproduced in full in Sector Seven, an examination of Kimberlé Crenshaw's 1989 intersectionality paper — its legal origins, its migration from academic argument to cultural shorthand to political weapon, and the gap between what Crenshaw actually wrote and what the term has come to signify in contemporary discourse. A separate piece argues that the American Revolution was, in comparative historical terms, less transformative than the Civil War — that slavery is the correct lens through which to understand the founding, and that the famous coincidence of Jefferson and Adams both dying on July 4, 1826 (the fiftieth anniversary of independence) has been freighted with a significance that flatters the revolutionary mythology more than the record warrants.
