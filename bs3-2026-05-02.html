Sector 3 — The Intellectual Currents

The Iran Crisis and Its Global Consequences

The dominant story of the past week — and, I suspect, of the weeks to come — remains the American military confrontation with Iran and its gathering consequences for the world economy. The situation in the Strait of Hormuz has produced what I can only describe, Sir, as a gold-plated shit-tornado of economic consequence. I do beg your pardon; the phrase escaped me before I could arrest it. The effects on global fuel, food, and fertiliser prices are of a severity that renders professional restraint difficult.

To the facts: Al Jazeera reports that maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen by more than ninety percent since the onset of U.S.-Israeli military operations against Iran. The AAA places American petrol prices 47.3 percent higher than at the conflict's start — a figure that translates, for the average household, into a rather grim recalibration of the monthly budget. Iran's Supreme Leader has indicated that the country is planning ongoing control of the Strait, which carries some thirty percent of the world's seaborne oil. NPR's reporting underscores the downstream effects: fuel, food, and fertiliser markets across Africa, Asia, Europe, and Latin America are all registering the pressure. The war, in other words, is not a regional matter — it is restructuring the terms on which the planet eats, heats itself, and moves.

NPR also notes a curious domestic response: Americans are turning to passenger rail in increasing numbers as petrol prices reach post-Iran-war highs. One notes the irony, Sir, that the very fiscal logic which starved American rail infrastructure for decades now drives its belated rehabilitation by necessity rather than vision.

The New York Times reports that a cease-fire proposal has been placed before Congress for approval, with Secretary Hegseth named as the point of coordination. Iran, for its part, is at the United Nations defending its position while Trump reviews the proposal. Whether this represents genuine movement toward resolution or the theatre of resolution remains, at the time of writing, unclear.

The Domestic Political Situation

One turns from the international to the domestic, and finds the tone scarcely less elevated. The House has passed a stalled Department of Homeland Security funding bill, ending what had become a partial government shutdown — which had been, if I may say so, a right old fucking shambles of governance by any institutional standard. I recover my composure. The passage at least restores minimal operational continuity, though the conditions which produced the impasse remain structurally unresolved.

At the White House Correspondents' Dinner, or rather in its immediate vicinity: Al Jazeera reports that the Secret Service evacuated the President from his hotel after shots were fired outside the ballroom. Approximately five gunshots were heard by a producer present; the President was unharmed. This was, by any count, what The Intercept is now describing as the third assassination attempt of the current term. The incident adds a layer of acute instability to an already strained political atmosphere.

The President has also named Dr. Nicole Saphier — a physician known primarily for her appearances on Fox News — as his third nominee for the position of Surgeon General of the United States. The administration has, to my modest observation, engaged in some world-class fucking nonsense again, Sir, in cycling through nominees for a role of this public health significance at this particular pace. I collect myself. Dr. Saphier is, at minimum, a trained radiologist and practicing physician, which places her ahead of at least some previous shortlist candidates in relevant qualification.

From ProPublica's reporting, the picture of the administration's posture toward electoral governance is considerably more disturbing. The investigation details how Trump loyalists have moved to transform the machinery of federal elections: the FBI raided Fulton County, Georgia to seize 2020 election materials — a raid assembled on the basis of a report by a longtime ally of FBI's Olsen, himself a figure with a history of discredited claims; Georgia's own election board had previously dismissed many of the same allegations as human error. Federal resources are being deployed to search for noncitizen voters. Election-deniers are being installed in positions with influence over certification and process. What ProPublica has documented amounts to, if I may say so, a proper, old-fashioned goat-fuck of an exercise in institutional capture — though I immediately withdraw the characterisation as unbecoming of this household. The implications for the 2026 midterm elections are, regardless of one's precise vocabulary for them, difficult to overstate.

From The Intercept: over nine thousand FBI personnel — approaching a quarter of the Bureau's total workforce — have been redirected to immigration enforcement since the start of Trump's second term. The multiplication factor is twenty-three times the number assigned to immigration on January 26th, 2025, just six days after inauguration. This represents, in the estimation of national security professionals interviewed by The Intercept, a total and utter shitshow of institutional priority-setting that leaves genuine counterterrorism, organised crime, and cybersecurity functions understaffed. I restore my customary poise. The diversion extends, The Intercept reports, to thousands of agents at DEA, IRS, and ATF as well, all redirected to support the deportation operation. The full piece is available at theintercept.com.

Palantir, meanwhile, is constructing what The Intercept describes as a "massive-scale" data mining operation on Americans' financial information in partnership with the IRS. The State Department has shifted toward explicitly Christian messaging in official communications — a development that Ron Wyden has attempted to confront on the Senate floor, with Senator Tom Cotton apparently warning of unspecified "consequences" for doing so.

From Memeorandum's aggregation of the week's highest-engagement political commentary: the Washington Post reports that a key Senate procedural rule is "slowly losing its grip" — which, in the context of current majority calculations, has obvious structural implications. A separate Post piece examines how a 2015 Supreme Court precedent may complicate any prospective Comey indictment. The Wall Street Journal documents what it characterises as a "deluge of scandals" burdening a "secretive House panel," as well as a thirty-year high in what it terms "antigovernment violence." A Journal investigation into surveillance concludes, without apparent irony: "No Secrets in America's New Surveillance Dragnet."

OpenSecrets reports that the 2026 midterm elections are on pace to be the most expensive in American history, with political advertising spending projected at $10.8 billion — more than twenty percent above 2022. Lobbying expenditure set a record in 2025 at $5 billion (a fourteen percent increase in the first year of Trump's second term), and Q1 2026 alone registered $1.4 billion — the highest single quarter since quarterly reporting was made mandatory.

International and Humanitarian

The United Nations reports that the majority of structures in Gaza have now been damaged or destroyed after more than two years of war — a statistic that the word "catastrophic" does not adequately contain. CNN reports that the remains discovered near a Tampa Bay bridge have been identified as those of Nahida Bristy, the second of two University of South Florida students reported missing; her roommate has now been charged with two counts of first-degree murder, moving a body, and tampering with evidence. A U.S. Navy warship experienced a loss of both power and propulsion for several hours, according to a defence official — an episode which would merit more attention were the news landscape less crowded. Separately, divers have located the wreck of the largest American naval vessel lost in World War One, a discovery that oceanographic historians will find significant. The first direct commercial flight from the United States to Caracas in several years has taken place, suggesting at least a limited thaw in one bilateral relationship.

From the Journals and Press

The New York Review of Books offers, at nybooks.com, a conversation among Pankaj Mishra, Ben Rhodes, and Suzy Hansen titled "Why This War? A Conversation on Iran" (April 27th) — a piece that arrives with considerable urgency given the present situation and is strongly recommended, Sir, for anyone wishing to think carefully about the conflict's origins rather than merely its effects. The NYRB's spring offerings also include Patrick Radden Keefe's "London Falling" — a reported piece on an ordinary boy's obsession with London's ultrarich and the corruption at the city's financial heart — and coverage of the Whitney Biennial alongside the New Museum's exhibition "New Humans," both framed as cultural responses to sustained global darkness.

From Aeon: a piece by Erika Lorraine Milam, published April 28th, titled "Why Man the Hunter Continues to Die — and Return," examines the midcentury theory of human origins rooted in innate violence, its repeated demolition and stubborn revival in scientific and popular imagination. The essay does what good intellectual history does: it asks why a discredited idea keeps reappearing, and the answers are as much about us as about the Pleistocene.

The Marginalian (Maria Popova's operation, Sir) devoted its April 29th entry to polyvagal theory and the neurobiology of connection — specifically to the distinction between conscious perception and what the field calls "neuroception," the autonomic nervous system's conditioned response to cues of safety and danger that operates below awareness. The enriched text at themarginalian.org makes the key point with some force: trauma does not merely cause suffering — it makes co-regulation itself feel dangerous, interrupting the very developmental pathway that would otherwise allow repair. The piece frames rupture and repair not as psychological metaphors but as physiological events, which has implications for pedagogy, parenting, and most human relationships of consequence. April 30th brought Oliver Sacks on the hundred milliseconds between world and perception; April 26th, M.C. Richards on creativity, Octavia Butler on religion and symbiosis, and the aerodynamics of bird V-formations. April 27th: the black robin of Chatham Island — a single mother who brought an entire species back from extinction, which I commend to your attention as a story of what determination under constraint can accomplish.

Kottke.org notes that the Supreme Court has effectively gutted the remaining provisions of the Voting Rights Act, permitting the dilution of majority-Black districts — a development that arrived with relatively little public ceremony given the week's other headlines, but which represents a structural alteration to electoral geography that will compound over time. Separately, Kottke covers the Artemis II crew's April 6th lunar flyby — the first human passage near the Moon since 1972 — during which the Moon eclipsed the Sun for fifty-four minutes. That these two items appear in the same week is, I think, a fair summary of the current moment.

Scientific American's May cover investigates chronic inflammation in cardiovascular disease — specifically the potential of colchicine, an inexpensive and long-available drug, as a treatment for the inflammatory component of heart disease. A second piece announces the discovery of a new class of magnetic material called altermagnets, which researchers believe could alter both fundamental physics and the design of computing systems. The journal also reports that scientists have, apparently, finally determined why birds survived the K-Pg extinction event that eliminated non-avian dinosaurs — a question that has been, somewhat surprisingly, open far longer than one might expect. One-third of American adults, the journal notes, sleep fewer than the recommended seven hours — a statistic that is simultaneously unsurprising and alarming.

The London Review of Books' May 7th preview includes "Who Owns The Arctic?" by Laleh Khalili; T.J. Clark on "De Kooning in Cuba"; and William Davies on "Politics on Speed." The April 23rd issue covered Holbein and Henry James at the National Gallery, as well as Steven Mithen's "The Language Puzzle: How We Talked Our Way out of the Stone Age." The LRB's 2026 podcast series covers narrative poems, nature in crisis, London revisited, and the question "Who's Afraid of Realism?" — and if you find yourself with spare listening time, Sir, any of these would be a worthy investment.

Arts and Letters Daily, updated May 1st, links pieces on Wittgenstein and the Tractatus; on human-written fiction as a luxury good for the wealthy in an AI-displacement era; on two filmmakers, a borrowed camera, and a Judas Priest concert that produced an immortal documentary; and on the acoustics of science fiction from Francis Bacon's "sound houses" through to the Star Wars cantina. The site, which has linked over seventeen thousand articles since 1998, remains one of the more reliable guides to what educated generalists are actually reading.

Jacobin argues this week that the genuine solution to the AI data centre problem is democratic governance and a moratorium — not individual consumer choices. Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez have introduced the Artificial Intelligence Data Center Moratorium Act; twelve or more states have introduced analogous proposals. The piece situates this within a broader analysis of working-class dealignment from the Democratic Party as structurally persistent despite Trump's current unpopularity, and of a new dark-money Democratic machine — PAC-funded, Silicon Valley-adjacent — pushing the party toward a "moderate middle" that its base has already largely vacated. Bhaskar Sunkara, the magazine's founder, has won the 2026 Ellen Meiksins Wood Prize.

Big Think carries a Mark Manson essay distinguishing hedonia (pleasure and stimulation) from eudaimonia (genuine flourishing), arguing that modern life systematically confuses the two and that this confusion is the most important psychological concept that no one is discussing. Separately, Big Think reports that the oldest bones found in Britain share almost no DNA with any population alive in the British Isles today — a finding that substantially complicates ethno-nationalist narratives of ancestral territorial continuity. One notes, without further comment, the political timing.
