Sector 7 — The New Yorker

New Yorker, May 4, 2026 — Talk of the Town (five pieces):

1. 'Donald Trump's Spring Cleaning' (Benjamin Wallace-Wells): Cabinet turnover — Pam Bondi fired as AG; revolving door of Trump's second-term personnel purges accelerating into spring 2026.

2. 'Reverend Billy Takes On Norman Foster's New Monolith': After midnight on Earth Day, Billy Talen dragged his pulpit to JPMorgan Chase's new Norman Foster-designed global HQ on Madison for a 24-hour sermon on fossil fuel ties. Stop Shopping Choir set up live stream in the rain. 'It's go-time, Billy,' said his wife/director Savitri Durkee. Now touring as Neil Young's opening act; writes for Young's Times-Contrarian. 'The only way we're going to start an effective movement is if each of us goes to the limit of our capabilities.' As night fell, voice dropped to a rasp: 'I'm not sure we cracked the culture. But we had a vision, and we did what we could.' ♦

3. 'Medallions, Movement, and Mamdani at MOMA PS1': Mayor Zohran Mamdani proclaimed 'My brother, we are in City Hall now!' to Richard Chow — a Myanmar cabdriver he hunger-struck with for 15 days in 2021 for taxi-medallion debt relief. Chow's brother Kenny's body found in East River 2018 after medallion debt.

4. 'Patrick Ball's Path to Broadway and Becky Shaw': Patrick Ball (The Pitt, NBC's acclaimed ER successor) appearing in Gina Gionfriddo's play Becky Shaw on Broadway.

5. 'Ellen Burstyn's Inner Library': Burstyn, 93, on a life organised around poetry. Her anthology 'Poetry Says It Better' spotted in NYC. Author talk with Pamela Anderson at The Westport Library for National Poetry Month. Poetry as a form of knowledge that outlasts memory.

These five pieces are, Sir, among the more humanly legible things I have encountered in this morning's reading. The Reverend Billy piece in particular — a man shouting into the rain outside a Norman Foster building about the end of the world, and then admitting quietly that he is not sure he cracked the culture — strikes me as an honest and rather dignified summation of a certain kind of public vocation. One does what one can. ♦
