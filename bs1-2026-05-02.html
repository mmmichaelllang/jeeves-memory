Sector 1 — The Domestic Sphere

Good morning, Sir. It is Saturday, the second of May, 2026, and I trust the night was restorative. The week has, as is often the case these days, deposited a rather considerable quantity of world upon our doorstep. I have sorted through the more consequential portions and set aside the remainder, as is my practice. Your coffee is, I presume, in hand. Let us begin.

Morning Correspondence

The morning post carried, among the usual circulars, a communication of a somewhat warming character — what might be described as a letter of greeting rather than substance. It read, in the portion that came to hand: "Good morning, Mister Lang. As we begin this new day, the morning sunlight casts a warm glow over the landscape, and I trust you are feeling refreshed and ready to tackle the day's challenges ahead."

I confess, Sir, that I found the sentiment not without merit, if somewhat broadly dispatched. There is something to be said for a correspondent who leads with encouragement rather than demand. The day does indeed appear, from the evidence available, to hold reasonable promise — and your household, in my estimation, is as prepared for it as any I have served. One takes encouragement where one finds it. I shall see that the remainder of the correspondence is held pending your afternoon leisure, as none of it appeared to require immediate reply.

The Weather

The meteorological situation this Saturday is, I am pleased to report, among the more agreeable the Pacific Northwest has lately managed. It is currently Mostly Sunny, with the thermometer at forty-five degrees Fahrenheit — a crisp and clarifying morning, Sir. The afternoon will bring a high of sixty-seven, which is frankly rather benevolent for this time of year and latitude. I would suggest the possibility of outdoor activity for the household, including the young miss, who will no doubt find the Marina Beach area agreeable.

This evening, conditions shift toward the partly cloudy, with lows near fifty degrees and north-westerly winds to ten miles per hour arriving by nightfall. It is, on balance, a May day that justifies optimism — that rare meteorological species in western Washington. A light jacket for the evening would be prudent, should you venture out.

Edmonds and the Surrounding Environs

I note several items of local moment, Sir, gathered from the municipal calendar and regional press.

The Edmonds Food Bank will be hosting an evening event on Thursday, the fourteenth of May, from six to eight in the evening, supported by local artists and — I confess I raised an eyebrow, but found the association entirely plausible — Rick Steves, the travel author and Edmonds resident, who has long been a patron of local civic causes. If you and Mrs. Lang have any inclination toward an evening of modest cultural philanthropy, this would appear a fitting occasion. One respects a man who returns his good fortune to the community in so direct a fashion.

From Edmonds-Woodway High School: Mathilda Hummel, a junior trumpeter, placed second at the Washington Music Educators Association All-State Solo competition in Ellensburg. This is, by any measure, a considerable achievement, and one trusts the school community has acknowledged it appropriately. The EWHS golf program holds a tournament on May ninth at Nile Golf and Country Club, should that be of any further domestic interest.

For those with civic inclinations, candidate filing for the 2026 ballot opens Monday, the fourth of May, at eight in the morning, and runs through Friday, the eighth, at five in the evening. If any household members are contemplating a venture into local governance — and I pass no judgment on such aspirations, Sir — the window opens shortly.

The Korean Association of Greater Seattle is hosting a job fair today, the second of May, at the Lynnwood Neighborhood Center. Represented sectors include technology, manufacturing, healthcare, aerospace, and vocational trades — a reasonably broad collection, though I note the absence of education as an explicit category, which perhaps reflects a gap in the organisers' imaginations rather than the market.

Public Safety — Edmonds (Three-Mile Radius)

Within the relevant geofence, I am pleased to report that there are no homicides, major assaults, armed incidents, or missing persons to communicate this morning. Edmonds maintains its standing as one of the safer municipalities in the region, with a composite crime score of 0.036 — which translates, for reference, to a grade of A. All neighbourhood areas — including Talbot Park, North Edmonds, and Perrinville-Seaview — carry ratings of A or A-plus. Year over year, violent crime has declined by 0.9 percent, though property crime has ticked upward by 14.6 percent, which bears watching without requiring alarm. The city remains well below national averages on both counts. The non-emergency dispatch line remains 425-407-3999 and online reporting is available at edmondswa.gov, should any routine matter arise.

It is, all told, a quiet morning here in our corner of Washington. One is grateful for that.
