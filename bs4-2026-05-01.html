Sector 4 — Specific Enquiries

Theological Physics and Triadic Ontology

The research apparatus has surfaced several developments of substance in the area of Trinitarian metaphysics and relational ontology, Sir, and I present them with the care they deserve.

Perichoresis.org is currently offering an eleven-week online course titled "Introducing the Trinitarian Faith 2026," consisting of twelve pre-recorded videos totalling approximately fifteen hours of instruction. The course represents a structured entry point for those seeking systematic engagement with Trinitarian theology in the academic mode.

Of considerably more specialized interest is the forthcoming Rome conference, scheduled for June 24–26, 2026, titled "Nature Lost, Nature Regained: Rethinking Nature Across Theology, Philosophy, and the Sciences." The conference is co-sponsored by New Trinitarian Ontologies (Austin), Triertium, and St. Mary Seminary — a coalition that suggests substantive interdisciplinary engagement rather than confessional in-fighting. The framing around the concept of "nature" positions this squarely at the intersection of your interests in triadic ontology and the philosophy of science.

On the academic side: Damiano Migliorini has published a paper on Academia.edu titled "Relational Ontologies and Trinitarian Metaphysics: Subsistences, Events and Gunk," in which the divine persons are construed as "subsisting relations" — a move that draws on Aquinas, Kant, and contemporary analytic theology in a genuinely ambitious synthesis. Erwin Schadel's triadic ontology has received renewed reconstructive attention via patristic grounding, and the PhilPapers Trinity bibliography now includes Beau Branson's 2022 contribution on the Monarchy of the Father and the analytic Trinity debate. These are, taken together, a respectable season's worth of primary literature for the serious student of the field.

Artificial Intelligence Systems — Reasoning Architecture

The frontier of AI systems research has, this week, produced findings of some technical density. Medium's 2026 analysis "Building Resilient Multi-Agent Reasoning Systems" argues that the agentic AI field has shifted its central question from "Can we?" to "How do we build systems that don't break in production?" — a maturation that practitioners in this space will recognise as hard-won. The analysis identifies weak reasoning architecture, rather than inadequate model capability, as the primary failure vector for production multi-agent systems. The frameworks of note — AutoGen, LangChain, LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGPT — are catalogued by SuperAnnotate as the leading multi-agent LLM orchestration tools.

Clarifai's 2026 ranking of open-source reasoning models merits close reading. The leading entries: GPT-OSS-120B; GLM-4.7 (approximately 42.8% on the Humanity's Last Exam benchmark, with noted strength in reasoning, coding, and agent tasks); Kimi K2 Thinking (a one-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts architecture); MiniMax-M2.1; DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen3-8B; DeepSeek-V3.2 Terminus; Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B; Qwen3-235B-A22B; MiMo-V2-Flash; and Ministral 14B Reasoning. The multiagent systems research thread at arXiv's cs.MA section remains active through April 2026, for those who prefer primary literature.

Pedagogical Innovation — AI in the Classroom

For the teacher-candidate dimension of your professional horizon: EdTech Magazine's January 2026 analysis identifies Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini, Grammarly, and Canva as the primary tools through which teachers are recovering meaningful time in their working weeks. SchoolAI's own research data indicates an average saving of 5.9 hours per week across six weeks — effectively returning a full working week to teachers annually. Eighty percent or more of teachers using AI for worksheet and assignment creation report significant time savings; 74% report savings in producing differentiated instructional materials.

The practical applications for the History/English classroom are increasingly well-documented. Edutopia highlights iCivics games for civics and history instruction; AI-facilitated interactions with historical figures as dialogic learning tools; and Minecraft-based environment building for ancient history units (with groups of two to three students constructing period structures). GradeWithAI offers a free history lesson plan generator built on the Gradual Release model (I Do/We Do/You Do), downloadable as PDF — a direct and low-friction resource. OpenEduCat claims a three-minute AI lesson plan generation cycle for history and social studies.

The EdTech Top 40 (Instructure/LearnPlatform, 2024–25) ranks the most-accessed K–12 educational technology by district. Cult of Pedagogy's current top picks include SchoolAI, Snorkl (AI-powered formative assessment), NotebookLM, Deck.Toys (game-like interactive lessons), WeVideo, and Read&Write. Jotform AI Agents and Curipod are circulating for classroom engagement applications. Google Classroom and Kahoot remain foundational. I note the Reddit history-teacher community's cautionary note: AI presents incorrect information with full apparent confidence, which makes teacher subject mastery not a supplement to AI use but a prerequisite for responsible deployment.

UAP Disclosure

The UAP disclosure situation continues to evolve in precisely the manner that the more experienced observers of these cycles have come to expect. Secretary Hegseth has, per DefenseScoop (February 25, 2026), doubled down on the Trump administration's disclosure pledge, assuring the press that the Pentagon will be "in full compliance" with the relevant executive order and that "more is coming on the UAP process." The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office is to deliver briefings and data releases.

In Congress, H.R.1187 — the UAP Transparency Act, now in the 119th Congress — would require the President to direct all federal agencies to declassify UAP records. The FY2026 National Defense Authorization Act includes three UAP-specific provisions: a Pentagon briefing to lawmakers on UAP intercepts by Northcom and NORAD since 2004; an AARO accounting of UAP classification guides; and related oversight language.

The Reddit r/ufo community's collective assessment, expressed with characteristic succinctness, is that the pattern is — and I quote the community's own characterization — "engaged in some world-class fucking nonsense again, Sir" — referring, one assumes, to the recurring cycle in which "every disclosure cycle begins with bold assurances, hearings are held, headlines surge, expectations rise," and the substantive declassification does not, in the end, materialise. I note the vulgarity only because the community's frustration is, on the historical record, not unreasonably founded, and I return to my more customary register: the institutional dynamics of classification make genuine disclosure exceptionally difficult regardless of executive intent.
