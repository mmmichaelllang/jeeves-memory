Sector 1 — The Domestic Sphere

Good Morning, Sir

Good morning, Mister Lang. I have taken the liberty of placing your tea on the side table — Earl Grey, two minutes steep, as you prefer — and drawing back the eastern curtains, through which a rather agreeable Monday light is currently making its unhurried entrance. It is the fourth of May, 2026, a day that opens with the kind of measured promise that the Pacific Northwest reserves for its better efforts: fifty-two degrees at this hour, with an anticipated high of seventy-six, and a sky that has not yet decided what opinion it holds of the morning. Time enough for that later.

The Morning Correspondence

The morning post arrived in its customary state, and I have arranged it in order of urgency before your chair. Allow me to summarise.

The correspondence leads with a collegial good morning and the observation that the morning sunlight casts what the sender characterises as a warm glow over the landscape. They trust — it is recorded with some warmth — that you are feeling refreshed and ready for the day ahead.

I am not entirely certain, Sir, that a warm glow constitutes intelligence in the journalistic sense, but the sentiment is noted with all appropriate appreciation. One does occasionally receive correspondence whose primary purpose is atmospheric encouragement rather than the conveyance of actionable material. The senders of such letters are, in my long experience, often well-meaning individuals who regard optimism as a form of courtesy — and they are not wrong. There are less agreeable ways to begin a Monday than a message from someone who wishes you well without asking for anything in return.

The remainder of the inbox was the usual miscellany of administrative notices, institutional communications, and the various concerns that require acknowledgment but not urgent reply. I have sorted these accordingly. Nothing that cannot wait until after breakfast, with one exception: the city council meeting tomorrow evening, which I address in the civic section below, has items on the agenda that touch on municipal appointments and finances. You may wish to form a view before Tuesday evening should you have any inclination toward public comment.

The tone of the morning correspondence — easy, collegiate, warm in the manner of the Pacific Northwest rather than the formal reserve of, say, a London law firm — reflects the kind of professional relationship that one maintains with equanimity. Should you wish to compose a reply before the afternoon's commitments, I would suggest keeping it brief and measured. The morning post has been attended to. The day's intelligence awaits below.

Weather — Edmonds, Washington, May 4, 2026

Today presents intervals of clouds and sunshine, Sir, with a high of seventy-six degrees Fahrenheit — what one might generously describe as the first genuinely hospitable afternoon of the spring proper. West winds at five to ten miles per hour will discourage any lingering April dampness from the garden paths. Tonight: clear to partly cloudy, with lows near fifty-two degrees. The extended outlook through the week holds much the same agreeable character: mainly sunny, highs in the mid-seventies, lows in the low fifties. Edmonds is, I observe, making a rather good meteorological show of itself at present. One is grateful for small mercies.

Municipal Affairs and Public Safety — Edmonds

Candidate filing for the 2026 ballot opened at eight o'clock this morning and runs through five o'clock on Friday, May eighth. Those citizens who have harboured political ambitions through the long winter months now have their moment of reckoning with the formal process. The City of Edmonds will be watching the filing window with its customary civic attentiveness.

The Edmonds City Council convenes tomorrow evening — Tuesday, May fifth — at six o'clock at the Public Safety Complex, 250 Fifth Avenue. The central agenda item is a vote on Mayor Rosen's appointment of Stephanie Lucach as the city's new administrator. Ms. Lucash currently serves as deputy city manager for the City of Kenmore; the proposed annual base salary for 2026 is $226,068. The Council will also take up a proposal to unpause the volunteer Edmonds Salary Commission for salary recommendations covering the 2027–2028 biennium — a total balls-up of the ledger, Sir, if I may say so, given the present state of municipal finances across the broader region. I beg your pardon. What I intended to convey, with rather more composure, is that the Salary Commission mechanism is, in principle, a sound instrument of democratic governance, and its reactivation is worth following if municipal compensation structures are of any interest to you.

On the matter of public safety: I am pleased to report there are no incidents warranting mention within the three-mile radius this morning, Sir. The most recent available data from the Edmonds Police Department rates the city with a crime grade of A-minus — violent crime has decreased 0.9 percent year-over-year, while property crime has increased 14.6 percent, consistent with regional trends and not, in my professional assessment, cause for material alarm in this neighbourhood. The average crime score of 0.036 places Edmonds comfortably in the upper tier of safety nationally, with all analysed neighbourhoods rated A-plus or A. For non-urgent matters: Edmonds Police Department, 425-407-3999.

Among the week's civic appointments: South County Fire Chief Shaughn Maxwell was sworn in to lead Snohomish County's largest fire agency, and Edmonds Police Chief Loi Dawkins promoted Commander Josh McClure to assistant chief. Both appointments reflect a degree of institutional steadiness that one finds rather reassuring in the present national climate.

The Snohomish Indivisible organisation continues its weekly protest gatherings at the Lynnwood Convention Center on Fridays from five to half past six in the evening. The May Day Strong protest last Friday drew approximately one hundred participants to Lynnwood — a modest but not insignificant turnout for a weekday demonstration. The YWCA Community in Bloom free event is scheduled for Wednesday, May thirteenth; a self-defence event is listed for May sixteenth. The Edmonds-Woodway High School golf tournament takes place Saturday, May ninth, at Nile Golf and Country Club — I mention it only as a traffic consideration should you be motoring in that direction, Sir.
