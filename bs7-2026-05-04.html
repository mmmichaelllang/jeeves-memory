Sector 7

From the current issue of The New Yorker:

A little after midnight on Earth Day, the street preacher Billy Talen dragged his pulpit to the new Norman Foster-designed global headquarters of JPMorgan Chase, on Madison. His self-ordained mission: deliver a twenty-four-hour sermon, calling attention to the bank's ties to the fossil-fuel industry. Talen, who is seventy-five, held an umbrella above his white leisure suit and silver bouffant, eying a pair of bank security guards. “Blessings upon your souls, gentlemen,” he bellowed. Members of his activist ensemble, the Stop Shopping Choir, were setting up a live stream in the rain. Savitri Durkee, who directs both the choir and Talen, who is her husband, clipped a mike to his lapel and checked her watch. “It’s go-time, Billy,” she said. “See you on the other side.”

One cannot help but note, Sir, that the juxtaposition of a seventy-five-year-old man in a white leisure suit, an umbrella, and a silver bouffant against the granite-and-glass solemnity of a Norman Foster bank headquarters is precisely the kind of scene that a novelist would hesitate to invent for fear of being accused of overwriting. And yet here it is, documented, in the rain, on Earth Day, in the pages of The New Yorker. The Stop Shopping Choir, one notes, knew exactly how to read the room. The bank, for its part, confined its response to posting a pair of security guards — a dignified, if ultimately inadequate, reply to a twenty-four-hour sermon delivered from the pavement in a white leisure suit. One respects the commitment on both sides.
