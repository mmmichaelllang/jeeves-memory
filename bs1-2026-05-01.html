Sector 1 — The Domestic Sphere

Good morning, Sir. It is Friday the first of May, 2026 — May Day, as the calendar insists on reminding us, though I confess the occasion carries rather different resonances depending on which end of the barricade one occupies. I trust you slept adequately. I have, as is my custom, been up since before the light, attending to the usual preparatory rituals, and am pleased to report that the household is, so far as I can determine, intact.

I have laid out your correspondence. There was a message of note — received this morning at 10:35, originating from your own address, as the research apparatus occasionally routes such items — which opened with the following observation, and I quote directly: "As we begin this new day, the sunlight casts a warm glow over the landscape, and I trust you are feeling refreshed and ready to tackle the day's challenges." I regret that the full body of the message was not returned in its entirety by the retrieval apparatus — a technical limitation I find characteristically inconvenient — but the spirit of the overture was, I think, sufficiently warm. There are no additional items of pressing personal correspondence requiring your immediate attention this morning.

The Weather

The morning air over Edmonds is, I am gratified to report, unusually cooperative for this time of year. At 5:28 this morning, the temperature stood at 51.1°F — that is 10.6°C for those who prefer the Continental notation — under clear skies. Humidity sits at 77 percent, which I note is entirely tolerable. A light north-northeast breeze of 2.2 miles per hour carries the salt off the sound with what I can only describe as a measured, almost courtly restraint. Visibility extends a full nine miles, and no precipitation is anticipated. Clear skies are expected to persist overnight as well. In short, Sir, if you were contemplating a constitutional this afternoon, conditions are, for once, not conspiring against you.

Edmonds — Municipal and Public Safety

Turning to local matters, the Edmonds Police Department's March 2026 operational summary has been published, and the figures speak to a community of some demand upon its constabulary: 2,124 calls for service were logged during the month, producing 250 case reports, 317 traffic stops, and 45 arrests. These numbers are, by any measure, the rhythms of an ordinary working police department rather than indicators of exceptional disorder. Annual reports remain available at edmondswa.gov, with longitudinal crime data accessible through the FBI Crime Data Explorer and the Washington Association of Sheriffs and Police Chiefs.

The April 13–19 police blotter from My Edmonds News recorded two incidents of particular note. An assault and theft were reported at the 23800 block of Highway 99 — at a motel in that corridor — and a disturbance described as an argument was logged at the 600 block of 3rd Avenue South, which is, of course, squarely within the downtown precinct. Neither incident appears to have resulted in injury of lasting severity, though the blotter naturally does not always furnish the full particulars. I mention these in the spirit of completeness.

There is also the matter of state-level oversight to address. The Washington State Auditor has, in a finding reported recently by My Edmonds News, determined that Washington's police departments lag on required training compliance. This is, to put it mildly, an absolute piss-take — though I collect myself immediately, Sir, and note merely that the State Auditor's concern appears legitimate and warrants the attention of any citizen who takes seriously the professional standards of public safety officers. Accountability mechanisms exist precisely for circumstances such as these, and one hopes the relevant agencies will respond with appropriate urgency.

On a rather more celebratory note from the Edmonds-Woodway High School community: trumpeter Mathilda Hummel secured second place at the Washington Music Educators Association competition held recently in Ellensburg. The achievement reflects considerable credit both on the young musician and on the school's music programme. I thought you and Mrs. Lang would wish to know.

The Edmonds City Council continues its regular schedule — meetings on the first, second, and fourth Tuesday of each month, held at the Public Safety Building at 250 5th Avenue North. The Council's April session addressed four proposed 2026 budget amendments along with personnel matters. The mid-biennium budget modification, it may be recalled, passed 5–2 in December last. The full agenda and meeting materials are available at edmondswa.gov/government/city_council.

Finally, for those in the broader Lynnwood corridor: the Korean Association is hosting a job fair tomorrow, Saturday the 2nd of May, which may be of peripheral interest depending on your network's needs.
