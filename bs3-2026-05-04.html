The War on Iran — Day 65

The situation in the Middle East remains the dominant thread of the morning's intelligence, Sir, now entering its sixty-fifth day. Several concurrent developments merit careful attention.

President Trump reported on Sunday, May third, that talks with Iran had proceeded with what he characterised as "very positive discussions," and that Washington is currently weighing a Tehran proposal to end the war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz. The practical pressure behind this diplomatic activity is not difficult to locate: the British Royal Navy has confirmed that maritime traffic through the Strait has declined by more than ninety percent since hostilities commenced. AAA reports US petrol prices have risen 47.3 percent since the war's outbreak; CNN places the current national average at $4.45 per gallon, representing approximately a fifty percent increase. The economic cost to the American consumer has become sufficient to concentrate minds at the negotiating level in ways that earlier geopolitical calculations had not.

The United States has begun dispatching naval assets to guide stranded commercial shipping through the Strait; two vessels have reported attacks in the course of that effort. Israeli forces conducted strikes on Syria's Quneitra and Daraa provinces, widening the active theatre beyond the immediate Iran engagement. The New York Times (May second) reports that Washington has fast-tracked arms deals valued at $8.6 billion to Mideast partners — a transaction that some will read as strategic consolidation and others as accelerant to a conflict whose termination is not yet assured. The New York Times (May second) also runs the wry judgment: "It's Only 5,000 Troops, but America Will Come to Regret Its Rash Withdrawal From Germany" — to which I turn below.

For those seeking analytical depth beyond the dispatch, the New York Review of Books published on April twenty-seventh a conversation entitled "Why This War? A Conversation on Iran," featuring Pankaj Mishra, Ben Rhodes, and Suzy Hansen — examining why this war began, the strategic miscalculations that preceded it, and its consequences for Iran, the region, and the broader world order. It is a piece of analysis worth reading slowly and in full, Sir, rather than in the abbreviated manner that the morning's schedule may encourage.

The Domestic Political Situation

Pamela Bondi has been relieved of her position as Attorney General of the United States — A right old fucking shambles, if one may speak with uncharacteristic directness. Forgive me, Sir; I momentarily lost my professional composure. What I intended to say is that her removal represents a significant transition in senior federal law enforcement leadership at a moment of unusual institutional pressure, with a Cabinet official reportedly under consideration as her replacement. A name has not been formally confirmed as of this writing.

The Washington Post (May first) reports that a potential Comey indictment may be overturned by a 2015 Supreme Court precedent — a development that will reward careful legal analysis rather than immediate conclusion. The Wall Street Journal ran a piece the same day under the headline "We Know You Live Right Here: No Secrets in America's New Surveillance Dragnet" — which, read alongside the confirmed extension of FISA Section 702, paints a portrait of surveillance infrastructure that has not materially contracted despite changes in administration, and indeed appears to have expanded its practical reach.

A federal appeals court has restricted access to mifepristone via telehealth and postal prescription — a ruling that continues the pattern of incremental erosion of reproductive healthcare access at the federal circuit level. The New York Times (May third) addresses a parallel erosion in the electoral domain in "Behind Voting Rights Act, a Clash Over the Reality of Racism."

ProPublica reports the Trump administration is engaged in a broad effort to reshape midterm electoral administration — what the outlet characterises as the systematic dismantlement of the guardrails that have historically insulated electoral processes from direct executive intervention. The Sebastian Gorka investigation, also at ProPublica, documents what reporters describe as a hollowed-out national security apparatus: gutted of institutional expertise in favour of ideological alignment. The Wall Street Journal (April twenty-seventh) ran what may be the headline of the year thus far: "The Year of the Molotov Cocktail: American Antigovernment Violence Hits a 30-Year High." That thirty years ago was 1996 is a historical comparison worth sitting with for a moment.

Spirit Airlines has grounded all operations — a total and utter shitshow of a corporate ending, if one may be briefly vivid. I apologise. What one ought to note, with appropriate analytical gravity, is that this is the first major US airline shutdown in twenty-five years, and it raises questions about the competitive structure of the budget carrier market and the adequacy of consumer protection mechanisms when an airline simply ceases to exist mid-booking-cycle for passengers who have paid forward. The practical consequences for travellers with forward reservations are considerable and not being resolved swiftly.

The Intercept reports that the FBI has multiplied the number of employees assigned to immigration enforcement by a factor of twenty-three in the first nine months of the Trump administration's second term — engaged in some world-class fucking nonsense again, Sir, when one considers the operational impact on the Bureau's other statutory responsibilities. I do apologise most sincerely for the language. The diversion of agents from the DEA, IRS, and ATF to immigration enforcement represents a reallocation of investigative capacity that will not pass without consequence in counterterrorism, financial crime, and public corruption work. The Intercept further reports that Palantir has received more than $130 million from the IRS for what is characterised as massive-scale data mining of sensitive federal databases — a gold-plated shit-tornado of a procurement decision, if I may be briefly forthright. I shall compose myself. The privacy implications are significant, and the institutional question of why a private technology firm possessing this access is appropriate has not been adequately addressed in the public record.

Representative Eric Swalwell has resigned from the House of Representatives following sexual assault allegations, per The Intercept.

Science and the Erosion of Knowledge Infrastructure

The Trump administration has terminated more than one hundred advisory committees serving the nation's science agencies, while simultaneously reducing the transparency mechanisms governing those that remain — A festering wound of pure fucking incompetence, Sir — I beg your pardon most sincerely. What I mean to convey, with the analytical sobriety the subject deserves, is that the systematic removal of independent scientific advisement is a form of epistemic self-harm whose consequences will compound with interest over the coming decade and whose reversal will not be swift, as institutional expertise once dispersed does not reconstitute on demand.

The FBI, per Scientific American, is now coordinating with the Department of Energy, the Pentagon, and state and local law enforcement to investigate possible connections between the deaths and disappearances of at least ten scientists with ties to government research projects. The House Oversight Committee has independently sought briefings from DOD, DOE, NASA, and the FBI. The Bureau has stated that no evidence of connections among the individual incidents has been established beyond the nature of the researchers' work and the fact that none of the incidents occurred before 2022. The most recent case involves retired Air Force Major General William Neil McCasland, who disappeared from his Albuquerque home in February, along with a gun and his wallet; his wife has written that it is "quite unlikely that he was taken to extract very dated secrets." NASA reports nothing in its records indicating a national security threat. The matter is, at minimum, unusual.

I note with interest, on a lighter scientific register: physicists are discovering new and structurally complex forms of ice — a reminder that the natural world yields genuine surprise independent of the news cycle. Scientific American also notes that individual dark points on a light wave can move faster than the wave itself, a finding that earns a moment of genuine appreciation even on a densely eventful Monday morning. The FDA has rejected the skin cancer drug RP1 twice. The Bill Gates-backed advanced nuclear plant under construction in Wyoming proceeds on its own timeline, as nuclear projects tend to do.

The World Beyond Washington

Myanmar's military government has transferred Aung San Suu Kyi from prison to house arrest. Her son, Kim Aris, expressed doubts about the gesture's significance — an assessment that, given the junta's record, seems well-founded. House arrest in Myanmar, under these particular authorities, is not meaningfully distinguishable from imprisonment in its practical effect on political agency or on the broader democratic situation.

Germany's defence minister has moved to downplay the Pentagon's announced withdrawal of five thousand American troops from German soil, characterising the reduction as manageable. The withdrawal has nonetheless rattled NATO allies, per NPR — a reaction that reflects the alliance's structural dependence on credible American forward presence. Any diminution of that presence, however modest in absolute terms, signals a trajectory whose endpoint is uncertain, and European partners already managing elevated threat perception from the east are recalibrating accordingly.

Hourly workers across the United States continue to navigate scheduling instability imposed by algorithmic management software — a structural condition that receives considerably less sustained analytical attention than its daily impact on the economic security of a substantial portion of the workforce warrants.

From the Literary and Intellectual Press

Aeon presents several essays this week that warrant your attention, Sir. Joao de Pina-Cabral's "To be is to participate" revisits Lévy-Bruhl's proposition that persons emerge from living with others rather than arriving at social life as pre-formed selves — a question with direct bearing on participation-based ontology and one that will not, I suspect, escape your notice given the directions of your current philosophical inquiry. Nicholas Humphrey's "The invention of the soul" argues that language itself created what we call the soul: not as discovery but as construction. Flora Champy's "Does reading do us any good?" takes on the relationship between literature and truth in a manner that a teacher of English has, one suspects, already formed a view about, but which benefits from being put to the pressure of argument. Vivek Venkataraman hunts down the "Man the Hunter" thesis in human origins scholarship — a century-old theory whose evidential support is, the piece suggests, considerably thinner than its persistence in the popular imagination implies.

The Marginalian (May third) presents Maria Popova's essay on Mary Oliver's reflection on the measure of a life well lived, drawn from Oliver's On Being interview with Krista Tippett and her reading of poems from Blue Horses, including "The Fourth Sign of the Zodiac." The site remains, as Popova notes, free, ad-free, AI-free, and fully human since 2006. In the present media environment, this deserves acknowledgment as an achievement of sustained character against considerable commercial pressure.

Kottke flags Richard Dawkins in conversation with UnHerd on whether AI represents the next phase of evolution — noting that his own exchanges with Claude and ChatGPT suggest he is encountering "highly competent intelligent beings." I offer no editorial position on that characterisation, Sir, except to observe that it has a certain recursive quality in this particular context. Kottke's "Farewell, Voting Rights Act" (April thirtieth) is, as its title suggests, not a cheerful read.

Arts & Letters Daily (May fourth) publishes a piece arguing that anxiety about artificial intelligence predates AI by several decades — historically accurate and perhaps usefully deflationary at a moment when the discourse tends toward the eschatological. The April thirtieth piece on Kierkegaard versus Copenhagen's tabloid press carries a mordant relevance to the present media climate. Jerry Saltz has unearthed 40,000 slides documenting the New York art world of the 1990s — a trove of considerable historical significance to anyone serious about recent cultural history, and worth following for the selections that will presumably begin circulating.

The London Review of Books marks its forty-fifth anniversary with a vinyl singles collection drawn from its poem archive, and announces a new "Close Readings" podcast pairing Jonathan Rée and James Wood — a reunion of philosophy and literature in close analytical conversation that those two disciplines have, in various institutional arrangements, long needed. Forthcoming issues feature James Butler on UK local elections and Tom Stevenson on Afghanistan. Published semimonthly and reliably Europe's leading magazine of ideas, it continues to reward the kind of reading that cannot be done in a hurry.

The New York Review of Books (May fourteenth issue) continues its Iran conflict coverage alongside an art issue featuring Jed Perl on the Whitney Biennial, a Guerrilla Girls piece, and writing on Seamus Heaney and on the Manet and Morisot pairing. The April twenty-ninth audio piece in their Private Life series features Maya Lin reading Martin Filler's "Ghosts in the House."

Jacobin's April 2026 cover piece, "Stop the AI Build-Out, Start the Fight," documents working-class community resistance to data centre construction in rural and semi-rural America — a story about energy infrastructure, local land use, and the question of who bears the environmental cost of the AI industry's physical expansion. A companion piece examines a widening class dealignment even as Trump approval craters amid war and tariff-induced inflation. The USC non-tenure-track faculty unionisation drive through United Faculty-UAW is described as potentially the largest such bargaining unit at a private university in the United States — a development worth following for anyone interested in the institutional conditions of academic labour.

OpenSecrets reports that Intuit and H&R Block spent more than seven million dollars in federal lobbying in 2025, chiefly in opposition to the IRS's free direct-filing programme — a figure that allows one to calibrate the distance between stated corporate concern for taxpayer interests and the behaviour that concern produces under pressure. ICE-tied technology and data analytics companies have sharply increased federal lobbying expenditure, a development of particular note given the concurrent expansion of immigration enforcement infrastructure documented earlier in this briefing.
