The Strait of Hormuz: A Crisis in Motion

The dominant story of the morning — indeed, of the past fortnight — continues to be the confrontation between the United States and Iran in and around the Strait of Hormuz, and events overnight have pushed what was already a fragile situation to what multiple sources this morning are describing, with unusual unanimity, as "the brink."

The United States has launched what the Trump administration is calling "Project Freedom," a naval operation aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial and military transit. In the overnight hours, US forces targeted Iranian boats operating in or near the strait. Iran, for its part, launched missile and drone attacks on the UAE — striking the VTTI oil terminal in Fujairah (jointly owned by IFM Global Infrastructure Fund, Vitol Group, and Abu Dhabi National Energy Co.) and damaging an ADNOC-affiliated tanker transiting the strait. Three Indian nationals were injured in the Fujairah strike. The UAE has condemned what it called an "unprovoked Iranian terrorist attack targeting civilian areas and facilities" and has indicated it is prepared to exercise its "full and legitimate right to respond."

The broader context, reported by Bloomberg: a US-Iran ceasefire had been nominally in effect since April 8th. Both sides fired shots. As of this morning, President Trump declined to confirm whether the ceasefire remains operative. The Guardian's live coverage carries Iran's warning — "We have not even begun" — as the lead. Al Jazeera reports that Iran has blamed "US military adventurism" for the new attacks and accuses the US of staging provocations to justify transit through the strait. A US official has separately stated that China is "funding" Iran, adding a layer of great-power framing to what is already a multi-party crisis.

The situation is — and I weigh my words carefully here, Sir — a total and utter shitshow. I withdraw the phrase immediately and replace it with: a crisis of considerable regional and global consequence, the trajectory of which remains deeply uncertain. US stocks are bouncing on optimism that the truce may yet hold; commodity markets are watching Hormuz with predictable anxiety, given that approximately 20% of global oil supply transits the strait.

Israel and Hezbollah have exchanged attacks in southern Lebanon, per Al Jazeera — a separate but related theatre of instability that the morning's dominant Hormuz story has somewhat overshadowed.

The Domestic American Scene

Ukraine: President Putin and President Zelensky have, with remarkable symmetry, each declared conflicting unilateral 72-hour ceasefires — neither recognising the other's announcement as legitimate. The New York Times notes this development with the dry observation that both leaders have stated terms the other finds unacceptable. The practical effect on the ground remains to be seen.

Voting Rights and Redistricting: The Supreme Court's ruling in the Voting Rights Act case has triggered what Politico and Memeorandum are describing as a "redistricting arms race." Alabama is holding a special legislative session specifically to redraw its congressional maps in response. NPR reports that the ruling is reverberating across multiple states, with partisan map-drawers on both sides moving quickly to take advantage of the new legal landscape. Memeorandum's morning aggregation lists this as among the top domestic stories, alongside separate coverage in the New York Times.

Mifepristone: The Supreme Court has granted mifepristone a one-week reprieve, restoring access via telehealth, mail, and pharmacies for the immediate term. The Associated Press and multiple outlets have this as a significant ruling; the underlying legal dispute remains unresolved. NPR's coverage emphasises the practical impact on patients in states where access has been restricted.

Rudy Giuliani: The former Mayor of New York and attorney is hospitalised in critical but stable condition, recovering from pneumonia. CNN reports he was removed from a ventilator on May 4th–5th. He is 81 years of age.

The Puerto Rico Prosecution: ProPublica this morning publishes an investigation into the gang "Los Tiburones" and a drugs-for-votes scheme in Puerto Rico. The gang mandated that prisoners show their ballots to gang leaders during both primary and general elections to avoid punishment; the indictment alleged connections with government officials aimed at reducing prison sentences. Investigators had, in ProPublica's assessment, the case "locked up." The Trump administration told prosecutors not to pursue charges. The DOJ's spokesperson stated the decision was "not about any election in particular." Puerto Rico's Civil Rights Commission reported "serious difficulties" gaining prison access during the 2024 general election. The piece names Resident Commissioner González-Colón in connection with the allegations. Trump has, in a separate context, called Puerto Rico "one of the most corrupt places on earth" — a characterisation that sits with some awkwardness alongside his administration's decision to halt the prosecution of alleged election manipulation there. This is, Sir — and I use the clinical language of political science — what one might term a proper omnishambles of the highest, most fucking degree. I do beg your pardon. A significant accountability failure, I meant to say.

Trump's Election Commentary: The New York Times carries an opinion piece under the headline "When You Think of It, We Shouldn't Even Have an Election." The President apparently made remarks to this effect. NPR reports that an Indiana election is being watched as a test of Trump's political influence at the state level as midterms approach.

The Keystone Light Pipeline: President Trump has approved a permit for what is being referred to as the "Keystone Light" Canadian pipeline. NPR has the details; the name is apparently not ironic.

Minneapolis and Immigration: NPR reports that Minneapolis-area immigrants continue to feel the effects of "Operation Metro Surge," a federal immigration enforcement crackdown. The Intercept provides broader context: the FBI has multiplied its immigration-focused employee count by 23 times in the first nine months of Trump's second term, redirecting 9,000 or more agents from national security work to immigration enforcement. Palantir holds a $130 million-plus IRS contract for "massive-scale" data mining. The State Department has shifted to explicit Christian messaging in its public communications. The FBI is also investigating possible links between the deaths and disappearances of at least ten scientists with government ties — the last item being reported by both the Intercept and Scientific American.

Blake Lively and Justin Baldoni: The parties have settled their lawsuit arising from the production and promotion of It Ends With Us. The New York Times carries the item. Terms were not disclosed.

The Life of the Mind — Journals and Intellectual Dispatches

The literary and intellectual press has had a productive week.

The New York Review of Books (May 14, 2026 issue) carries Coco Fusco on Wifredo Lam — described as a meditation on Lam's synthesis of European modernism with Afro-Cuban consciousness, titled "A Vital Unconscious." Jed Perl contributes "Art for Our Age of Chaos," reviewing the Whitney Biennial alongside the New Museum's exhibition "New Humans: Memories of the Future." Mark O'Connell reviews Patrick Radden Keefe's London Falling in a piece called "London's Brutal Underground." Online, Daniel Lefferts offers "Mystery Brain" (published May 2nd). The current table of contents is available at nybooks.com.

The London Review of Books (Vol. 48, No. 8) features Laleh Khalili's "Dr Freezelove: Who Owns the Arctic?" — a history spanning from the 1867 Alaska purchase to the present geopolitical contest over Arctic resources and shipping lanes. Lorraine Daston contributes "Everywhere and Nowhere: Climate Proxies," examining the use of proxy data in climatology, sociology, and economics, including the historical deployment of such proxies to justify racial hierarchies. T.J. Clark writes on De Kooning; William Davies on what he terms "hyperpolitics."

Aeon publishes Charles Foster's "Why creativity shines out on the edge of things" — an argument that brilliance and generativity emerge at margins rather than centres, drawing on nature as its primary evidence. A separate piece, "Why reality is more than the sum of its particles," explores emergent entities such as phonons as patterns rather than elementary objects. A third piece draws on Lévy-Bruhl's concept that personhood emerges from living with others — "To be is to participate."

The Marginalian (Maria Popova, May 3rd) publishes a brief piece described as "a small poem about our largest learning: how to forgive." May 5th also marks Annie Dillard's 81st birthday; Popova's post on Dillard's concept of "unselfconsciousness" is worth noting: the argument that "identities and opinions are the least interesting things about us." Dillard at 81 remains, by all evidence, the kind of writer who makes one feel one ought to spend more time paying attention.

Kottke.org reports that Boards of Canada are returning with a new album; listening parties are scheduled for May 22nd in New York City, Tokyo, and London. A 55-minute mix of B-sides and rarities has been posted online. Jason Kottke also links a piece arguing that Tracy Kidder's The Soul of a New Machine "does a better job challenging AI hype than most current criticism" — a claim that is, in my view, worth sitting with rather than dismissing.

Arts & Letters Daily (May 5th) leads with "AI anxiety predates AI," which I take to mean that the philosophical and psychological disquiet now attributed to machine intelligence has roots in prior anxieties about automation, media, and modernity. The site also features Kierkegaard versus the Copenhagen tabloid press — a matchup more relevant to present conditions than one might initially suppose — and Jerry Saltz's 40,000 slides documenting the 1990s New York art world. The question of why science fiction imagines alien music as it does — traced from Francis Bacon's "sound houses" to the Star Wars cantina — is among the livelier pieces.

BigThink offers Mark Manson's argument that modern life confuses comfort and stimulation with fulfilment, framing the hedonia/eudaimonia distinction as "the most important psychological distinction" in contemporary life — a claim one cannot wholly dismiss. BigThink also covers a finding that the oldest bones in Britain share almost no DNA with anyone alive today, which is offered as a direct refutation of ethnic "homeland" claims. Artemis II raises questions about NASA and NSF science funding amid the Iran war news cycle.

Jacobin publishes "Stop the AI Build-Out," documenting working-class community resistance to Big Tech data centre construction — with Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez's AI Data Center Moratorium Act as the legislative vehicle. A separate piece addresses class dealignment as a structural phenomenon rather than a transient reaction to Trump. Bhaskar Sunkara has won the 2026 Ellen Meiksins Wood Prize.

OpenSecrets projects $10.8 billion in political advertising spending for the 2026 midterms — more than 20% above 2022 levels. First-quarter 2026 lobbying totals $1.4 billion, the highest quarterly figure since reporting was mandated. ICE-tied technology companies have sharply increased their lobbying activity. The Campaign Legal Center and OpenSecrets jointly won an FEC lawsuit on January 30th, 2026.

Scientific American: Beyond the FBI scientist deaths investigation, the journal reports that individual dark points on a light wave can move faster than the wave itself — a counterintuitive finding in wave optics. A new class of magnetic materials called "altermagnets" has been discovered. Scientists now have an account of why birds survived the K-Pg extinction event that killed the non-avian dinosaurs.
